Design Considerations

Audio

Additional Edit

While recording, my microphone generated a noticeable amount of noise. I suppressed this noise with Audacity's Noise Reduction effect. The provided audio asset in the Audio Recreation section above already has noise reduced.

Accessibility

According to W3C's recommendations, the volume of foreground sounds must always be 20 decibels louder than that of background sounds. For this reason, the volume of the background music is low throughout during the speech to make it audible.

Background Music Decision Process

I wanted backing music that mildly evoked tension; was not jarring; and, as such, not distracting to users. AVBE's work met all the criteria.

The process by which I chose my image assortment is fairly trival. Other than limiting my options to copyright-free images, there was only one aspect that influenced my decision: relevance. The sebsequent sub-sections how select images are relevant to the topic at hand.

Encountered Challenge

The only notable challenge I faced was captioning the video. For optimal accessibility, I decided to add closed captions (CC) to my video. This task is monotonous if done manually. I had to ensure the caption cues were in sync with the video's audio (i.e., my narration) so they remained relevant and, thus, helpful to users. Fortunately, I used a video editor called VEED.IO, which sports a subtitle generator. The tool expedited the captioning process as I used the generated file as a starting point.

Despite the initial assistance, when incorporating my changes, I accidentally introduced inconspicuous syntax errors because of my unfamiliarity with Web Video Text Tracks (abbreviated as WebVTT), the file format for describing captions. Although the syntax is relatively straightforward, mistakes can be difficult to spot because HTML5's <track> element silently ignores an imperfect VTT imperfect file. For debugging assistance, I resorted to validation tools such as Subtitled Pro's Subtitle Validator, which uncovered the problems.
